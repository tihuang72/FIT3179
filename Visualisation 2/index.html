The Need for Renewable Energy

The use of fossil fuels - coal, oil and natural gas has been essential in the development of society as we know it today. Fossil fuels are used to generate heat, power vehicles and to generate electricity amongst other many other uses.

However, fossil fuels are the primary cause of greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions which have been linked to climate change, extreme weather conditions and other changes in nature. Additionally, the extraction, processing and burning of fossil fuels has also caused air and water pollution. These impacts have accelerated the need to transition towards renewable energy.

The scatter plot compares the amount of CO2 emissions in tonnes with the percentage of energy a country generates that is renewable. It describes the trend that more renewable energy generation leads to less CO2 emissions

Historically Australia has been a large exporter of raw materials. This trend includes that of fossil fuels with there being a coal and natural gas mining boom in the early 2000s. Australia's top 3 exports include iron ore, coal and natural gas.

The area chart on the right describes the export of fossil fuels in Australia over time. Australia exports ~75% of the energy it generates.

The large increase in fossil fuel exports has given rise to concern over its effect on the emissions outside of Australia.

Renewable Energy Efforts

The Paris agreement in 2016
Kyoto Protocol in 2007

As part of the Paris Agreement, Australia pledged to reduce emissions by 43% by 2030 compared to 2005 levels and achieve net zero emissions by 2050.

Australia notably has increased energy generation from solar and wind. In 2023, renewable energy made up a record 35% of all energy generated.

A Focus on Solar Energy in Australia

The major areas of renewable energy growth have been in the expansion of wind and solar energy, particularly small-scale solar. Small-scale solar refers to solar energy systems with a capacity of less than 1 MW, primarily including solar panels installed on residential properties or businesses.

Residential solar has been incentivized by the Small-scale Renewable Energy Scheme, which aims to promote the installation of small-scale solar, wind, or hydro systems by subsidizing the upfront installation costs.

The map highlights the small-scale solar potential (MW) in local government areas (LGAs) in Australia, categorized by solar energy sources. Additionally, the blue markers indicate the locations of large-scale solar plants currently operating in the eastern part of the country.

Residential solar energy has significant growth potential, driven by advancements in solar technology and increasing adoption rates among Australian households.

Latest efforts and the future

Projections show that by 2030 Australia is on track to reduce greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions by 42% by 2030, 1% less than the Paris agreement target. The Australian government has also set a 82% renewable energy target for 2035.

However, despite the increase in renewable energy generation the majority of energy consumption is still from fossil fuels. Only Tasmania has a large proportion of renwable energy use. Australia aims to remedy this issue with investment in new technology such as large batteries to store excess energy
